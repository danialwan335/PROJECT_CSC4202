# 2.2 Route Value Prioritization

## What Makes One Route "Better" Than Another?

In this scenario, the sole optimisation objective is **minimising total travel time in minutes**. However, multiple factors influence what travel time is assigned to each road segment (edge weight). This section explains how edge weights are determined and why travel time is the correct value to optimise.

## Why Travel Time, Not Distance?

During a post-landslide emergency:
- Two road segments of equal **length** (e.g., 5 km each) may have vastly different **travel times** — a paved estate road at 60 km/h takes 5 minutes, while a waterlogged logging track at 10 km/h takes 30 minutes.
- Road conditions, gradient, surface quality, debris clearance, and visibility in the dark all affect how fast the ambulance can safely travel.

Travel time is therefore a more accurate proxy for medical urgency than physical distance.

## Edge Weight Assignment

Each road segment in the graph is assigned a travel time weight based on:

| Factor | Effect on Weight |
|---|---|
| Road surface (paved/unpaved) | Paved roads → lower time |
| Road gradient | Steep gradient → higher time |
| Post-landslide debris | Partial debris → higher time |
| Distance between nodes | Longer segment → higher time |
| Estimated safe speed | Higher safe speed → lower time |

The weight formula for a road segment is:

> **w(u, v) = distance_km / safe_speed_kmh × 60** (in minutes)

For example, a 3 km unpaved logging road with a safe speed of 20 km/h yields:
w = 3 / 20 × 60 = **9 minutes**

## Optimisation Priority

Since the goal is to minimise total patient transfer time, the algorithm assigns higher "priority" (i.e., explores first) to paths with lower cumulative travel time. There is **no multi-factor priority formula** in this problem — travel time is the single, unambiguous optimisation criterion. This simplicity is appropriate because:

1. In a medical emergency, every second counts — we do not trade off between competing business objectives.
2. The graph model is already a compression of all real-world factors into a single time estimate per segment.
3. Introducing secondary optimisation criteria (e.g., fuel cost, road risk) would complicate the model without improving the fundamental life-saving outcome.
